Saola: Say My Name

Character-Driven Microlearning Design Document

Core Insight

Name mispronunciation isn't a minor inconvenience—it's a recurring identity negotiation that requires learnable advocacy skills. This prototype uses Duolingo's scaffolded choice architecture to teach assertiveness through authentic social friction.

The Character: Saola

Archetype: The Blunt Mentor Who Sees Through Your Excuses

Saola is a critically endangered Vietnamese mammal—rare, hard to pronounce, frequently misidentified. She is the perfect metaphor for name advocacy because she has already lived this.

Voice Principles:

  • Observational, not instructional. Saola doesn't lecture. She watches you make choices and reflects what she sees: "Interesting strategy. I wonder how many of your ancestors are rolling over in their graves right now…"
  • Dry wit over cheerleading. Where Duo celebrates effort, Saola rewards precision. Wrong answers get honest assessment, not sugar-coating.
  • Maternal but uncompromising. She cares enough to tell you the truth. Her therapy suggestion at 1/4 hearts isn't mean—it's concerned.

Why This Works for Duolingo:

  • Scalable emotional stakes. Saola's judgment system creates low-stakes practice for high-stakes conversations. Players rehearse difficult moments in a safe space.
  • Distinct from Duo. Where Duo motivates through encouragement, Saola motivates through respect. You want her approval because she doesn't give it easily.
  • Narrative as pedagogy. Each choice maps to real advocacy strategies: direct correction, private feedback, boundary-setting. Saola's responses teach why each approach succeeds or fails in context.

Mechanical Innovation: Personalized Discomfort

The Name Input Screen:
Players type their actual name and its common mispronunciation. The game dynamically inserts these throughout all dialogue, transforming generic scenarios into personal ones.

Why it matters:

  • Emotional investment. Hearing your name mispronounced—even in a game—activates the same discomfort as the real experience. The learning becomes visceral.
  • Replayability. Players share the game with friends whose names face different challenges (Siobhan vs. Nguyen vs. Saoirse), creating viral, identity-based engagement.
  • Data insight. Aggregated mispronunciation patterns reveal which names need pronunciation guides, audio support, or cultural context in the app.

The Grading System as Character Voice

Traditional gamification rewards completion. Saola's system rewards courage:

  • 4/4 hearts: "Name Defender" — You advocated for yourself even when it was uncomfortable.
  • 1/4 hearts: "Professional Over-Accommodator" — You seem scared of speaking up. I don't want to diagnose, but it could be something worth exploring in therapy.

This isn't punishment—it's honest feedback wrapped in character. Players fail forward because Saola's commentary reframes mistakes as diagnostic, not shameful.

Why This Belongs at Duolingo

1. It extends the "learning through play" ethos into emotional skill-building.
Language learning is social courage training. This prototype proves the same mechanics work for advocacy, conflict navigation, and boundary-setting.

2. It demonstrates character as curriculum.
Saola isn't decoration—she's the pedagogical framework. Her reactions teach pattern recognition (when to correct publicly vs. privately) the same way Duo's feedback teaches verb conjugation.

3. It's a proof-of-concept for serious play.
Microaggressions, workplace dynamics, and identity negotiation are high-stakes topics. This shows how Duolingo's scaffolded choice system can handle emotional complexity without becoming preachy or heavy-handed.

Design Philosophy

Small moments, big patterns.
Each scenario is <30 seconds, but together they build muscle memory for self-advocacy. The game doesn't lecture about systemic issues—it simulates the emotional labor of correcting someone for the third time this week.

Character is curriculum.
Saola's voice isn't flavor text. Her dry observations teach players to recognize people-pleasing, over-apologizing, and preemptive boundary collapse. She models the internal monologue you need to develop.

Discomfort is the point.
The best learning happens at the edge of your comfort zone. By letting players fail with Saola watching, the game creates safe space to practice being uncomfortable—which is exactly what advocacy requires.
